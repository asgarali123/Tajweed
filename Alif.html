Letter Alif (ا) and Hamzah (ء) - Tajweed Rules

This video explains the proper pronunciation of Alif and Hamzah.

Makhraj (Articulation Point)

Unlike Hamzah (ء), the letter Alif (ا) is not a letter which takes a vowel or pronounced. The letter Alif (ا) is not used on its own in the Arabic language. Instead, it is mostly used to stretch a sound, like making an "ah" sound longer. The actual letter that represents the flat "ah" sound is called Hamzah (ء). In the Usmani script (used in many Qur'ans), you will see the letter Hamzah written in words. However, in the Indo-Pak script, Hamzah is often left out. That’s why, in this chapter, we will learn about both Alif and Hamzah so you can understand their differences and uses.

Alif (ا)

  • Alif is pronounced from the empty space in the mouth (Jowful Fam).
  • It has no sound unless it stretches the letter before it with a fatha. Also known as Hooruful-Madd.

Hamzah (ء)

  • Hamzah is pronounced from the deepest part of the throat (Aqsal Halq).
  • It has a clear "ah" sound like in the word أَكَلَ (akala).
  • If you put your hand on your neck and say "ah," you will feel the vibration deep in your throat. That’s where Hamzah is coming from!

Mouth formation for the letters Alif (ا) & Hamzah (ء)

Alif (ا)

  • Alif before which is a Fatha gives an "Ah" sound as in apple.
  • Yaa before which is a Kasrah gives an "E" sound as in Egypt.
  • Waaw before which is a Dhammah gives an "Oo" sound as in food.

Hamzah (ء)

  • Fatha - The mouth is made with a smile
  • Kasrah - The mouth is made flat but also a smile
  • Dhammah - The mouth is made round

Sifaat (Characteristics)

Alif (ا)

  • Not a speaking letter.
  • Main Role: Lengthens the sound of the letter before it.
  • Does not possess Hams, Qalqalah, Jahr, or Shiddah.

Hamzah (ء)

  • A speaking letter with clear sound.
  • Primary Sifaat:
    • Jahr: Clear sound.
    • Shiddah: Complete stoppage of sound.
    • Istifal: Tongue remains flat.
    • Infitaah: Mouth remains open.
  • No Hams, No Qalqalah.

Tajweed Rules

Rules for Alif (ا)

  • Alif is part of Huroof al-Madd (stretching letters).
  • The elongation depends on the type of madd:
    • Natural Madd (Madd Tabee’i): 2 counts.
    • Secondary Madd (Madd Far’ee): Can extend to 4, 5, or 6 counts, depending on the rules.
  • It only works when preceded by a fatha.
  • It cannot begin a word because it cannot carry a vowel.

Rules for Hamzah (ء)

  • Hamzah is a speaking letter with a sharp, distinct sound.
  • Hamzah can appear in three positions:
    • Beginning: أَكَلَ (akala)
    • Middle: يَسْأَلُ (yas'alu)
    • End: شَيْءٍ (shay'in)
  • Types of Hamzah:
    • Hamzah al-Qat’: Always pronounced.
    • Hamzah al-Wasl: Only pronounced at the start of a word.
  • If two Hamzahs appear together, the second Hamzah may be softened.

Stretching Rules for Alif (ا) According to Shatibiyyah

These rules explain how Alif is stretched in the recitation of Hafs 'an Asim based on Shatibiyyah.

4 or 5 Counts:

  • Madd Munfasil (Separated Madd): When a Madd letter (ا, و, ي) is followed by a Hamzah (ء) in the next word.
  • Madd Muttasil (Connected Madd): When a Madd letter is directly followed by a Hamzah (ء) in the same word.

6 Counts (Mandatory):

  • Madd Laazim (Obligatory Madd): When a Madd letter is followed by Shaddah (ّ) or Sukoon (ْ).

Summary:

  • 4-5 Counts: Madd Munfasil & Madd Muttasil.
  • 6 Counts: Madd Laazim (mandatory).
