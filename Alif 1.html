Letter Alif (ا) and Hamzah (ء) - Tajweed Rules

This video explains the proper pronunciation of Alif and Hamzah.

Makhraj of Alif (ا) and Hamzah (ء)

Makhraj of Alif (ا):

Alif is a stretching letter, not a speaking letter, so it doesn’t have its own sound or place of articulation like Hamzah.

  1. Where does Alif come from?
    • Alif doesn’t have its own independent makhraj. Instead:
      • It stretches the sound of the letter before it.
      • Alif works only if the letter before it has a fatha (a small diagonal line above a letter, making an "a" sound).
  2. How does it sound?
    • Alif makes the "ah" sound longer.
    • Example: In the word قال (qaal), The Qaaf (ق) has a fatha ("qa") and the Alif stretches it, making it "qaaa."
  3. How to feel it?
    • If you put your hand on your neck and say "ah," you will feel the vibration deep in your throat. That’s where Hamzah is coming from!
  4. Alif is known as Hooruful-Madd when before it is a fathah and is Jowful-Fam, meaning, pronounced from the emptiness of the mouth

Makhraj of Hamzah (ء)

Where does Hamzah come from?

Hamzah is a speaking letter, and it has a specific place in the throat where it is pronounced.

  1. Where does Hamzah come from?
    • Hamzah is pronounced from the deepest part of the throat (called the aqs al-halq in Arabic).
    • This is the very bottom of the throat, near where the throat meets the chest.
  2. How does it sound?
    • Hamzah makes a sharp "ah" sound, like the sound you make when you say the English word "apple" or the Arabic word أَكَلَ (akala, meaning "he ate").
  3. How to feel it?
    • If you put your hand on your neck and say "ah," you will feel the vibration deep in your throat. That’s where Hamzah is coming from!
  4. It is pronounced from Aqsal Halq, meaning, the part of the throat nearest to the chest.

Understanding the Letters Alif and Hamzah

The letter Alif (ا) is not used on its own in the Arabic language. Instead, it is mostly used to stretch a sound, like making an "ah" sound longer. The actual letter that represents the flat "ah" sound is called Hamzah (ء). In the Usmani script (used in many Qur'ans), you will see the letter Hamzah written in words. However, in the Indo-Pak script, Hamzah is often left out. That’s why, in this chapter, we will learn about both Alif and Hamzah so you can understand their differences and uses.


Understanding Alif (ا):

Alif is a Helper Letter:

  1. Alif doesn’t make a sound on its own. It’s like a helper that makes the "ah" sound longer when it comes after a letter with a fatha (a small diagonal line above a letter).
    Example: In the word قال (qaal), the Alif makes the "qa" sound longer: "qaaaal."
  2. Alif Never Starts a Word:
    Alif cannot be at the beginning of a word because it doesn’t carry a vowel (no fatha, dammah, or kasrah).


Understanding Hamzah (ء):

  1. Hamzah Makes a Sound:

    Hamzah is a real letter. It makes a sharp "ah" sound, like in the word أَكَلَ (akala), meaning "he ate."

  2. Hamzah Can Be at the Start, Middle, or End:
    • Start: In أَكَلَ (akala), Hamzah starts the word.
    • Middle: In يَسْأَلُ (yas'alu), Hamzah is in the middle.
    • End: In شَيْءٍ (shay'in), Hamzah is at the end.
  3. Two Types of Hamzah:
    • Hamzah Al-Qat' (Important Hamzah): This Hamzah is always pronounced clearly.
    • Hamzah Al-Wasl (Joining Hamzah): This Hamzah is pronounced only if it starts a word. If it’s in the middle, it disappears when joining words.

    Example:

    • Starting: اِذْهَبْ (idhhab) → Hamzah is pronounced.
    • Joining: وَاذْهَبْ (wadhhab) → Hamzah disappears.
  4. Two Hamzahs Together:

    If two Hamzahs are next to each other, the first one is pronounced normally, and the second is made soft or replaced with a long sound.

Hamzah and Alif in the Qur'an:

  1. In Usmani Qur'ans: You will always see Hamzah written clearly when it’s part of a word.
  2. In Indo-Pak Qur'ans: Sometimes, Hamzah isn’t written, but we still pronounce it.

Why is This Important?

  • Knowing these rules helps us recite the Qur'an properly.
  • Hamzah tells us when to make a sound, and Alif helps us stretch sounds.

Easy Tip:

Hamzah speaks. Alif stretches. Think of Hamzah as a talker and Alif as someone pulling the sound longer!


Sifaat of Hamzah (ء) and Alif (ا)

Hamzah (ء):

Hamzah is a speaking letter with specific characteristics. It is pronounced clearly and sharply, making it distinct from other letters.

Sifaat of Hamzah (ء):

  1. Primary Sifaat (Without Opposites):
    • Sifat Al-Jahr (جهر): Hamzah is pronounced with strength and clarity because the sound is stopped, and the air doesn’t flow freely.
    • Sifat Al-Shiddah (شدة): Hamzah is one of the "strong" letters, meaning the sound is completely stopped during its articulation.
    • Sifat Al-Istifal (استفال): The tongue stays flat, and there is no elevation in the pronunciation.
    • Sifat Al-Infitah (انفتاح): The sound flows out without compression, and the mouth remains open.
    • Sifat Al-Ismat (إصمات): Hamzah is a non-liquid letter that is not suitable for being root letters of many Arabic words.
  2. Secondary Sifaat (With Opposites):
    • Hams (No): Hamzah does not have the quality of hams because it does not allow air to flow during its articulation.
    • Qalqalah (No): Hamzah does not have qalqalah because it is not one of the five qalqalah letters (ق ط ب ج د).

Alif (ا):

Alif is a madd (stretching) letter. Unlike Hamzah, it does not have its own unique sound and is dependent on the letter before it for pronunciation. It does not carry sifaat in the same way as other speaking letters because it is a helper (lengthening) letter.

Qualities of Alif (ا):

  1. Primary Qualities:
    • Alif has no sound of its own: It stretches the sound of the letter before it when the letter has a fatha.
    • Alif depends entirely on the letter before it: If there is no fatha before it, Alif is silent or doesn’t appear in recitation.
  2. Not a Speaking Letter:

    Since Alif does not articulate on its own, it has no hams, qalqalah, or other speaking-letter qualities.

  3. Special Characteristics:
    • Alif always creates madd (elongation).
    • The elongation depends on the type of madd:
      • Natural Madd (Madd Tabee’i): 2 counts.
      • Secondary Madd (Madd Far’ee): Can extend to 4, 5, or 6 counts, depending on the rules.

Conclusion:

  • Hamzah: A sharp, clear letter with distinct qualities that make it easy to identify.
  • Alif: A helper letter with no sound of its own, used only to elongate the sound of other letters.
